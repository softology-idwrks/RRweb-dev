write ABOUT THE STUDY AT JOHNS HOPKINS READING 1600 WPM

In studies conducted at Johns Hopkins University Wilmer Eye Institute a group of participants accelerated their reading speed from an average of 200 wpm to more than 1600 words per minute with comparable comprehension. These were ordinary subjects and there was no training or practice involved. How did they do it? The experimenters changed the method of reading from moving the eye from word to word across a page of text to displaying the words to the subjects as a sort of "movie". The results were immediate and astonishing. By streaming the words in a sequence one at a time in the middle of the field of vision the ability to read was instantly multiplied. What this showed and has been repeatedly confirmed is that when it comes to reading speed it's not our comprehension that slows us down. It's actually our inability to see the words faster. It's about our eye muscles and the process of capturing the words on the page.

What it comes down to is that us are still using a method TO READ that hasn't advanced in 5,000 years. By using technology in the lab to change the presentation of the words the limits of having to rely on slow, exacting and tiring eye movement were removed. Our brains were free to do what they do best, see something and quickly process the information we receive rather than waiting for our eyes to catch up.

RSVP is a bit like a machine gun flashing words at you like bullets. It's inherently uncomfortable. The challenge was to make the technology assist in a natural, comfortable way. This led to the insight of using the rhythm of natural speed to modulate the way the words were presented. Just like someone would speak. We are hardwired for speech and those rhythms contribute significantly to comprehension as well as ease. The Devil was however in the Details. What were those cadences? How do you make a computer display words in a way that wasn't robotic? The answer lay in analyzing the speech patterns of real humans and mimicking the way they used cadence. This wasn't the stiff formality of punctuation or simply the length of words but rather a flexible foundation which changes continually with the speed of presentation. It took nearly 10 years of trial and error, refinement and sampling to arrive at a truly natural experience of rhythm in language. This process of refinement continues at SoftOlogy constantly improving and adapting to accelerate written communication. There's a lot more to the story. A written word is simply a symbol that has a meaning. Once we learn that meaning our brains can recognize their shapes almost immediately.

We are extremely efficient at recognizing patterns and visual symbols. We do it almost instantaneously every day. Once we know what a symbol means we can recognize and process it at extremely high speeds. For our ancestors this skill was important for survival. Today it may mean not walking into the wrong bathroom. The issue isn't recognizing or comprehending but rather the mechanics of reading that interferes.

In the clinical setting they found out that there is an important advantage to moving the text to the center of our field of vision and displaying it to us like a movie. What happens is that we recognize the words for what they actually are symbols. Like a stop sign. We don't need to sound them out to know what they mean... all we have to do is look.

 

TALK ABOUT THE COMFORT FACTOR AND RHYTHM BEING THE KEY TO MAKING IT PRACTICAL

As we develop a "sight vocabulary" The association between the symbol and meaning is automatic. This translates into much higher reading speed.

To further support the research findings in another study, subjects were given a brain scan while reading in this fashion. The result was surprising. It turned out that a completely different part of the brain lit up. It wasn't the area linked to reading and language it was the area called the visual cortex. This is where we process visual information including symbols. And our brains are very fast at pattern recognition. We are much faster at identifying symbols than we are at converting them into language and then sounding them out. That's why RapidReader Works so well. It simply accommodates the display of words to the way we most naturally process symbols. It's sort of "cuts out the middleman" and allows us to understand directly from what were seeing. RapidReader's speed isn't "voodoo" it’s simply the practical application of real science refined through development of a technology to solve a problem.
